Answer (TL;DR)

Sinek argues that some arenas are “infinite games”: there’s no finish line, players and rules can change, and the goal is to keep playing and advancing a Just Cause by practicing (1) Just Cause, (2) Trusting Teams, (3) Worthy Rivals, (4) Existential Flexibility, and (5) Courage to Lead.[2], [1] Mathematics fits this pattern: there are always more problems, methods evolve, and progress is measured by contributions, not trophies—think open problem lists (e.g., the Millennium Problems), long-horizon programs (Langlands), and community collaborations (Polymath).[4], [6], [5]

Reason Why (Evidence)

On Sinek’s official book page, the “five practices” are explicitly listed and described: Just Cause, Trusting Teams, Worthy Rival, Existential Flexibility, and Courage to Lead. These operationalize an infinite mindset for leaders.[2]

The framework extends Carse’s finite vs. infinite games distinction: finite play seeks to win; infinite play seeks to continue play.[3]

Mathematics is intrinsically open-ended: institutionalized unsolved-problem lists (e.g., CMI’s Millennium Problems) highlight that the frontier stays open and the “game” continues.[4]

Modern collaborations like the Polymath Project demonstrate distributed, ongoing play across many contributors—fitting an infinite game’s ethos.[5]

Multi-decade research programs—such as the Langlands program linking number theory, automorphic forms, and geometry—are paradigmatic “just causes” that outlast individuals and redefine subfields over time.[6]

The community shows “existential flexibility”: e.g., acceptance of computer-assisted proofs (Four Color Theorem) and paradigm-shifting methods in number theory (Wiles’s proof of Fermat via modularity).[8], [7]

    Math Track: Mapping the Five Practices

    1. Just Cause → Truth-seeking & unification. In math, the “cause” is deeper understanding (e.g., unifying conjectures like the Langlands program). The payoff is explanatory power, not a final “win.”[6]
    2. Trusting Teams → Peer review & open collaboration. arXiv, journals, seminars, and community efforts (like Polymath) create psychological safety for ideas and error-correction.[5], [11]
    3. Worthy Rivals → Parallel programs & competing methods. Rival approaches surface blind spots (e.g., combinatorial vs. analytic routes in major problems).[9], [10]
    4. Existential Flexibility → Method shifts. Embracing computer-assisted proofs (Four Color Theorem) or new bridges (modularity lifting in Wiles/Taylor).[8], [7]
    5. Courage to Lead → Publishing unpopular ideas & pursuing long bets. Long-horizon work often precedes validation; progress may arrive years later via others’ breakthroughs.[7]
    Metric shift: In an infinite game, success ≠ “be the best.” Instead: contribute, connect, and keep the play going—pose better questions, prove new theorems, refine definitions, enable others.

    Themes (with a mathematics lens)

    1. From scorekeeping to stewardship. Swap leaderboard thinking for contribution thinking in math culture and education.[3]
    2. Game design matters. Incentives (prizes, tenure, credit) should encourage open problems, robust replication, and shared infrastructure (e.g., arXiv), not zero-sum contests.[4], [11], [12]
    3. Programs outlive players. Big agendas (e.g., Langlands) show why institutions must outlast individual careers.[6]
    4. Flexibility beats dogma. When tools change (e.g., computer proof assistants), infinite players adapt the “how” to protect the “why.”[8]

    Exemplars & Case Studies

    • Millennium Problems (CMI). A curated frontier that institutionalizes open-ended play; one problem solved (Poincaré), many to go.[4]
    • Polymath Project. Massively collaborative problem solving born on blogs; demonstrates “trusting teams” at field scale.[5]
    • Langlands program. A unifying “just cause” threading number theory and representation theory across decades.[6]
    • Fermat’s Last Theorem (Wiles & Taylor). A landmark win that emerged from a method shift (modularity), illustrating courage and flexibility in service of a cause.[7]
    • Four Color Theorem. First major computer-assisted proof; a cultural turning point on what “counts” as proof.[8]

    Glossary (quick reference)

    Just Cause
    A future state so compelling it warrants sacrifice; in math, durable knowledge and unification of ideas.[2]
    Trusting Teams
    Environments of psychological safety enabling learning and error-correction (seminars, peer review, collaborations).[2]
    Worthy Rival
    Another approach or group that reveals our weaknesses and spurs improvement.[2]
    Existential Flexibility
    Capacity to make profound strategic shifts (e.g., new proof paradigms) to advance the cause.[2]
    Courage to Lead
    Holding to the cause despite short-term pain; backing ideas whose value may be long-term.[2]
